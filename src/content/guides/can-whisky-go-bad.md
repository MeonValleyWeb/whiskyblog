# Can Whisky Go Bad?

The short answer: **unopened whisky doesn't spoil**, but **opened bottles slowly degrade over time**. Unlike wine, whisky doesn't continue to mature once bottled, but oxidation can affect its flavour.

---

## Unopened Whisky: Decades of Shelf Life

An unopened bottle of whisky, stored properly, can last indefinitely. The high alcohol content (typically 40% ABV or higher) acts as a preservative, preventing bacterial growth and spoilage.

**Key points:**
- Store upright (not on its side like wine)
- Keep away from direct sunlight
- Maintain consistent temperature
- Avoid extreme heat or cold

---

## Opened Whisky: The Oxidation Clock Starts

Once you open a bottle, oxygen begins to interact with the whisky. This process is gradual and doesn't mean the whisky becomes "unsafe" to drink—it just means the flavour profile may change.

**Timeline for opened bottles:**
- **First 3-6 months:** Minimal change, some argue slight improvement as whisky "opens up"
- **6-12 months:** Noticeable mellowing, alcohol bite may soften
- **1-2 years:** More pronounced changes, some complexity may fade
- **Beyond 2 years:** Significant flavour loss, especially in bottles less than 1/4 full

---

## What Causes Whisky to Deteriorate?

### Oxidation
The primary culprit. As whisky is exposed to air, volatile compounds evaporate and chemical reactions occur, gradually flattening the flavour profile.

### Light Exposure
UV light can break down compounds in whisky, leading to off-flavours. This is why many premium bottles use dark glass.

### Temperature Fluctuations
Extreme heat can accelerate oxidation and even cause expansion that damages the seal. Cold won't "spoil" whisky but can cause cloudiness (chill haze).

### Cork Degradation
Old or dried-out corks can crumble or allow more air into the bottle, speeding up oxidation.

---

## Signs Your Whisky May Have Gone Off

While whisky rarely becomes "bad" in a food safety sense, here are signs of degradation:

- **Faded colour** (especially in lighter whiskies)
- **Loss of aroma intensity**
- **Flat or muted flavour**
- **Unpleasant musty notes** (if cork has degraded)
- **Visible sediment or cloudiness** (not always a problem, but worth noting)

---

## How to Preserve Opened Whisky

**Best practices:**

1. **Keep bottles upright** – Prevents cork contact with alcohol (which can degrade the cork)
2. **Store in a cool, dark place** – Cupboard or cabinet away from windows
3. **Minimize air exposure** – Consider decanting into smaller bottles when less than 1/4 full
4. **Use wine preservation systems** – Argon or nitrogen gas can displace oxygen
5. **Seal tightly** – Ensure the cap or cork is secure after each pour

**Special considerations for collections:**
- For bottles you plan to keep long-term, consider vacuum pumps or preservation sprays
- Wax-dipped seals can help prevent evaporation
- Avoid storing in kitchens (temperature fluctuations) or bathrooms (humidity)

---

## The Bottom Line

Whisky won't "go bad" in the way milk or meat does, but an opened bottle will slowly lose character over months and years. For the best experience:

- Drink opened bottles within **1-2 years**
- Store properly to maximize shelf life
- Don't stress too much—even "degraded" whisky is usually perfectly drinkable
- If a bottle has sentimental value, consider preserving it with inert gas

Unopened bottles? They're nearly immortal with proper storage. That 30-year-old bottle inherited from your grandfather is likely still excellent.
